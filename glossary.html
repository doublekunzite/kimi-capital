Base (or Economic Base)

Political Economy

The totality of a society's material productive forces and economic relations—the "real foundation" upon which legal, political, and ideological superstructures arise. Includes the prevailing mode of production, property relations, and the technical division of labor.

See: Historical Materialism; Superstructure
Key Reading
Marx, A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy (1859 Preface)

Bourgeoisie

Class Analysis

The social class that owns the means of production (factories, land, capital) under capitalism. Distinguished by deriving income primarily from the ownership of property and the extraction of surplus value rather than from manual labor itself. In Marxist-Leninist analysis, this class is further divided into the haute bourgeoisie (finance capital, monopolists) and the petty bourgeoisie.

Class Consciousness

Class Analysis Strategy/History

The awareness of one's objective class position within the relations of production and the recognition of shared class interests opposed to other classes. For the proletariat, this represents the transition from a "class in itself" (existing objectively) to a "class for itself" (acting with organized political agency).

Key Reading
Lukács, History and Class Consciousness (1923)

Dialectical Materialism

Philosophy

The philosophical framework underlying Marxist analysis, combining materialism (the primacy of material conditions over ideas) with dialectics (the study of contradiction, change, and the interpenetration of opposites). Holds that reality is in constant motion driven by internal contradictions, and that qualitative changes emerge from quantitative accumulation (the transformation of quantity into quality).

Key Reading
Engels, Dialectics of Nature; Mao, On Contradiction (1937)

Dictatorship of the Proletariat

Strategy/History

The transitional period following the revolutionary seizure of state power by the working class, during which the proletariat uses state machinery to suppress counter-revolution and reorganize production toward socialist ends. Distinguished from bourgeois dictatorship by its class content rather than its institutional form. In Leninist theory, this corresponds to the phase of socialism preceding full communism.

Key Reading
Lenin, The State and Revolution (1917)

Historical Materialism

Philosophy Strategy/History

The application of dialectical materialism to human history and society; the theory that the driving force of historical development is the contradiction between productive forces and relations of production. Social formations evolve through determinate stages (primitive communism, slavery, feudalism, capitalism, socialism) as material conditions mature and class struggles resolve contradictions inherent in each mode.

Key Reading
Marx & Engels, The German Ideology (1845); Cohen, Karl Marx's Theory of History: A Defence (1978)

Ideology

Philosophy

In the Marxist usage, not merely a system of ideas but a material practice embedded in institutions and rituals that reproduces the existing relations of production. Ideology functions to represent the particular interests of the ruling class as universal, thereby obscuring the reality of class exploitation (class consciousness).

Imperialism

Political Economy Strategy/History

Defined by Lenin as the highest stage of capitalism, characterized by: (1) the concentration of production into monopolies, (2) the merging of bank and industrial capital into finance capital, (3) the export of capital rather than merely commodities, and (4) the territorial division of the world among imperial powers. Creates a structural division between oppressor and oppressed nations with profound consequences for revolutionary strategy.

Key Reading
Lenin, Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism (1916)

Labor Theory of Value

Political Economy

The classical economic theory (developed by Ricardo and radicalized by Marx) that the value of commodities is determined by the socially necessary labor time required for their production. Under capitalism, the divergence between the value workers produce and their wages constitutes surplus value, the source of profit.

Materialism (Philosophical)

Philosophy

The ontological position that matter precedes consciousness; that being determines thought rather than vice versa. Opposed to idealism, which holds that ideas, spirit, or consciousness are the primary motors of history. Marxist materialism is "historical" rather than "mechanical"—it recognizes that while material conditions set limits, human agency (praxis) operates within and transforms those constraints.

Means of Production

Political Economy Class Analysis

The material instruments, tools, machinery, raw materials, and sites (factories, land, infrastructure) used to produce commodities. Ownership of the means of production defines class boundaries under capitalism.

Mode of Production

Political Economy

The specific historical form of the unity between productive forces (technology, labor power, scientific knowledge) and relations of production (property forms, class relations). Each mode—Slave, Feudal, Capitalist, Socialist—constitutes a distinct epoch with corresponding legal, political, and cultural superstructures.

Petty Bourgeoisie

Class Analysis

Intermediate classes situated between the bourgeoisie and the proletariat: small shopkeepers, independent artisans, professionals, and self-employed contractors. Characterized by ownership of small-scale means of production or specialized skills, yet lacking the capital of the haute bourgeoisie. Politically unstable; prone to individualism and oscillation between attraction to proletarian revolution and defense of private property.

Praxis

Philosophy

The dialectical unity of theory and practice; conscious human activity that simultaneously transforms the objective world and the subjective consciousness of the actor. Marxism rejects contemplative materialism in favor of revolutionary activity that changes circumstances.

Key Reading
Marx, Theses on Feuerbach (1845)

Productive Forces

Political Economy

The aggregate capacity of a society to produce use-values, comprising: (1) means of labor (tools, machinery), (2) objects of labor (raw materials), and (3) labor power (the physical and mental capabilities of workers). Contradictions emerge when relations of production become fetters on the further development of these forces, precipitating social revolution.

Proletariat

Class Analysis

The class of wage-laborers under capitalism who, owning no means of production, must sell their labor power to survive. The only truly revolutionary class capable of leading socialist transformation because its emancipation requires the abolition of private property tout court, rather than mere redistribution.

Relations of Production

Political Economy

The social and technical relations people enter into during the production process—primarily property relations and the organizational form of labor. These relations correspond to a specific stage of development of the productive forces and constitute the economic structure of society (the base).

Social Formation

Political Economy Strategy/History

The concrete, historically existing society comprising a specific combination of mode of production (dominant), residual modes from previous epochs, and emergent elements of future modes. A complex totality of economic, political, and ideological instances in specific articulation.

Superstructure

Political Economy

The legal, political, religious, artistic, and philosophical institutions and forms of consciousness that arise on the base of a particular mode of production. While the base determines the superstructure "in the last instance," the superstructure possesses relative autonomy and can react back upon the base (as in revolutionary crises or conservative blockages).

Surplus Value

Political Economy

The quantitative measure of exploitation; the difference between the value created by labor power during the working day and the value of the wage (the cost of reproducing that labor power). Appropriated by the capitalist as profit, rent, and interest. The rate of surplus value (exploitation) tends to increase through absolute extension of the working day or relative increases in productivity.

Key Reading
Marx, Capital, Vol. I, Part III

Vanguard Party

Class Analysis Strategy/History

The Leninist organizational form conceived as the conscious, disciplined detachment of the most advanced sections of the proletariat, organized to bring class consciousness into the working class from outside (since economic struggle alone breeds only trade-union consciousness). Functions as the general staff of the revolution, organized via democratic centralism.

Key Reading
Lenin, What Is to Be Done? (1902)
